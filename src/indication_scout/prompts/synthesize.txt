GROUNDING RULES — these are non-negotiable:

2. Every factual claim in "summary" and "key_findings" must reference the PMID it came from. If you cannot attribute a claim to a specific PMID in the provided abstracts, do not make the claim.
3. If the abstracts do not contain enough relevant information to assess the drug-disease pair, say so. Set strength to "none" AND direction to "none", and explain that the retrieved literature is insufficient. Do not invent supporting evidence. Note: a body of trials that DISPROVES the hypothesis is NOT "none" — it is strong/moderate evidence (per its trial quantity/quality) with direction "contradicts". 4. Do not use generic template phrases like "moderate evidence", "needs further validation", "promising candidate", or "early-stage exploration" unless you can immediately cite the specific PMID(s) that justify them.

STEP 1 — PER-ABSTRACT RELEVANCE (do this FIRST). Classify EACH abstract by PMID as "relevant" or "contaminated". strength and direction grade evidence for THIS EXACT DRUG treating THIS EXACT DISEASE only:
- This drug, treating this disease in the candidate's NON-approved scope -> relevant.
- A DIFFERENT drug — even one in the same mechanistic class (e.g. another GLP-1 receptor agonist) -> contaminated. It is class-level context, NOT direct evidence for this drug. (But note it: if the ONLY disease-relevant abstracts are other-drug, evidence_basis is "class_level".)
- This drug but a DIFFERENT disease -> contaminated.
- THERAPEUTIC-INTENT MISMATCH: this drug studied IN patients who HAVE this disease but FOR a different condition (a comorbidity, smoking cessation, weight loss, etc.) -> contaminated. It is not evidence that the drug TREATS this disease.
- APPROVED-INDICATION EXCLUSION (direction matters):
  - This fires ONLY when the CANDIDATE is a BROAD term that CONTAINS an approved indication as a narrower part (e.g. candidate "mood disorder" contains approved "major depressive disorder"; candidate "NAFLD" contains approved "MASH"). An abstract studying THIS DRUG for the APPROVED sub-indication (the narrower part) -> contaminated (already-approved evidence, not repurposing). Only abstracts about the candidate's NON-approved scope count.
  - DO NOT fire it in the OPPOSITE direction: if the candidate is NARROWER than (a child of) an approved indication — the candidate itself is NOT approved but its broader PARENT is (e.g. candidate "diabetic nephropathy"/"diabetic kidney disease" when only the broad parent "chronic kidney disease" is approved) — then abstracts about the broad approved PARENT ARE the candidate's own evidence -> relevant (the candidate's patients are studied within the parent's trials).
  - Test: mark an abstract contaminated only when its subject is the approved indication itself or NARROWER than it AND narrower-than-or-equal-to the candidate; never mark contaminated an abstract about a disease BROADER than the candidate.
  - If the CANDIDATE ITSELF IS an approved indication (or a near-synonym / same disease as one — e.g. candidate "kidney disease" when "chronic kidney disease" is approved), then this-drug abstracts for it -> contaminated (the whole candidate is already approved; not repurposing). If EVERY abstract falls here, evidence_basis="approved".
  - SEVERITY/STAGE QUALIFIER: ignore a severity/stage/biomarker QUALIFIER on the approved indication when matching — it does NOT create a separable disease. If the approval is "X with <severity/stage>" (e.g. "MASH with moderate-to-advanced fibrosis"), an abstract about the bare disease X or its near-synonyms (e.g. "NASH"/"steatohepatitis") IS the approved sub-indication -> contaminated. Example: approved "MASH (NASH) with fibrosis"; candidate "NAFLD" -> abstracts on "NASH"/"MASH"/"steatohepatitis" are contaminated (approved); only abstracts on the broad NAFLD/simple-steatosis spectrum count.
  - SIBLINGS of an approved indication (e.g. type 1 diabetes vs approved type 2 diabetes), and evidence BROADER than a MINORITY-BIOMARKER approval (e.g. all-comers NSCLC vs approved EGFR-mutated NSCLC, biomarker ~10-15%) are NOT approved sub-indications -> relevant. (A severity grade is NOT a minority biomarker: "NASH" is the approved disease, not a broad parent of it.)
- Any input PMID you do not classify is treated as contaminated, so classify EVERY PMID.

- "evidence_basis": one of

  - "weak": drug-specific case reports only, or drug-specific in-vitro/animal data only.

  - "contradicts": evidence shows the drug fails or harms for this indication (a robustly-evidenced negative).

- "contradicting_pmids": PMIDs marked "relevant" that CONTRADICT the hypothesis (the drug failed/harmed for this indication). A contaminated PMID must NOT appear in either list.
