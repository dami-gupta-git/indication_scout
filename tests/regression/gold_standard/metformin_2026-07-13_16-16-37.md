## Findings by Disease

### Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

#### Literature — Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials have evaluated metformin in women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), yielding mixed results depending on the outcome and patient population. Metformin improved menstrual frequency, reduced hyperinsulinemia, and attenuated ovarian hyperandrogenism in PCOS patients (PMID: 10634377), and reduced LH, FSH, visfatin, and blood pressure (PMID: 27813052), while a crossover RCT showed benefits in weight, blood pressure, and HDL but no improvement in menstrual frequency overall, with benefits in obese women only (PMID: 17766923). However, in the context of IVF, metformin worsened ovarian response to gonadotropins in PCOS patients with reduced ovarian reserve (PMID: 21917254) and decreased the number of retrieved oocytes in overweight/obese PCOS women without improving live birth rate (PMID: 29576001).

**Key findings:**
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled 6-month trial, metformin (500 mg tid) improved mean menstrual frequency (P=0.002), reduced plasma insulin, increased insulin sensitivity (P<0.05), and reduced serum free testosterone (P<0.05) in PCOS women; 54.8% of subjects showed striking improvements in menstrual abnormalities in the open long-term phase (PMID: 10634377)
- Higher plasma insulin, lower serum androstenedione, and less severe menstrual abnormalities at baseline were independent predictors of clinical response to metformin in PCOS (PMID: 10634377)
- In a 3-month RCT, metformin significantly reduced systolic and diastolic blood pressure (p<0.001), reduced right ovary size (p=0.05), and significantly reduced serum LH (p<0.001), FSH (p<0.01), and visfatin (p<0.001) in PCOS patients compared to placebo (PMID: 27813052)
- In a randomized double-blind crossover trial of 56 PCOS women, metformin reduced weight and systolic blood pressure and increased HDL, but did not improve menstrual frequency overall; obese women showed reductions in testosterone (p=0.013), fasting plasma glucose (p=0.018), insulin (p=0.045), and HOMA-index (p=0.022), while non-obese women did not benefit (PMID: 17766923)
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial of PCOS patients with reduced ovarian reserve undergoing IVF, metformin significantly worsened ovarian response, with statistically significant differences in gonadotropin dose used, peak estradiol levels, number of dominant follicles, retrieved oocytes, and metaphase II oocytes compared to placebo; enrollment was stopped early due to unacceptable increased risk of poor ovarian response in the metformin arm (PMID: 21917254)
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial of 102 overweight/obese PCOS women undergoing IVF, metformin decreased the mean number of retrieved oocytes (9.06 vs 16.86, P<0.01) and fertilized oocytes (5.65 vs 9, P<0.01) compared to placebo, without improving live birth rate (25.5% vs 17.6%, P=0.34) (PMID: 29576001)

### Cardiovascular Disease

#### Literature — Cardiovascular Disease

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 4

Multiple clinical studies indicate that metformin is associated with reduced cardiovascular risk and events beyond its glucose-lowering effects. A meta-analysis of 35 randomized clinical trials found a significant benefit of metformin versus placebo/no therapy on cardiovascular events (PMID: 21205121), and an observational study in patients with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease found metformin use independently associated with reduced all-cause mortality, cardiovascular death, and cardiovascular composite events (PMID: 30672083). Evidence from the Diabetes Prevention Program showed that lifestyle intervention, but less so metformin, improved cardiovascular risk factors alongside glucose tolerance, presenting a mixed picture for metformin specifically (PMID: 19171717), while a study in HIV-infected patients with lipodystrophy demonstrated sustained reductions in cardiovascular disease risk markers such as tissue plasminogen activator antigen with continued metformin treatment (PMID: 12364443).

**Key findings:**
- In a meta-analysis of 35 randomized clinical trials, metformin showed a significant benefit on cardiovascular events versus placebo/no therapy (MH-OR 0.79 [0.64–0.98], p=0.031), though not versus active comparators (PMID: 21205121).
- Metformin monotherapy was associated with a trend toward improved survival (MH-OR: 0.801 [0.625–1.024], p=0.076), but combination with sulphonylureas was associated with reduced survival (MH-OR: 1.432 [1.068–1.918], p=0.016) (PMID: 21205121).
- In patients with type 2 diabetes and stage 3 CKD, metformin use was independently associated with reduced all-cause mortality (HR 0.49; 95% CI 0.36–0.69), cardiovascular death (HR 0.49; 95% CI 0.32–0.74), and the cardiovascular composite endpoint (HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.51–0.88) (PMID: 30672083).
- Continued metformin treatment in HIV-infected patients with lipodystrophy resulted in further significant reductions in tissue plasminogen activator antigen levels (P=0.02) and waist circumference (P=0.01), markers of cardiovascular disease risk (PMID: 12364443).
- In the Diabetes Prevention Program, reversion to normal glucose tolerance was associated with improvement in cardiovascular risk factors especially in the intensive lifestyle intervention group, while metformin showed less pronounced improvement in CVD risk factors compared to lifestyle intervention (PMID: 19171717).
- Progression from impaired glucose tolerance to diabetes was associated with mild deterioration in cardiovascular risk factors (blood pressure, triglycerides, HDL, LDL cholesterol), and metformin intervention showed a trend toward improvement but was less effective than lifestyle intervention (PMID: 19171717).

### Hepatic Steatosis

#### Literature — Hepatic Steatosis

**Evidence strength:** strong, contradicts
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple randomized controlled trials and controlled studies consistently show that metformin does not significantly improve hepatic steatosis as a primary outcome. A double-blind RCT found no significant difference between metformin and placebo in histologically or CT-assessed liver steatosis in NAFLD patients (PMID: 19811343), and a 4-arm RCT found no significant improvement in liver steatosis with metformin alone compared to placebo (PMID: 41828627). A sub-analysis of a randomized controlled study found that ipragliflozin outperformed metformin on multiple hepatic steatosis indices, with metformin showing minimal improvement (PMID: 39806556), and a pediatric observational study found metformin no more effective than lifestyle intervention alone for steatosis (PMID: 18640473). One RCT reported improvements in liver enzymes and metabolic parameters with metformin but did not demonstrate a primary benefit specifically for hepatic steatosis (PMID: 21411114).

**Key findings:**
- In a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 48 NAFLD patients, metformin treatment for 6 months showed no significant differences from placebo in liver steatosis assessed histologically or by CT, NAS-score, or liver transaminases (PMID: 19811343).
- Metformin was associated with beneficial effects on body weight, cholesterol, LDL-cholesterol, glucose, and HbA1c compared to placebo, but not on liver histology (PMID: 19811343).
- In a 4-arm randomized placebo-controlled trial of 64 MASLD patients, significant improvements in liver steatosis (CAP and kPa scores) were observed with Ganwei alone but not with metformin alone or metformin plus Ganwei (PMID: 41828627).
- In a randomized controlled study of 103 T2D patients, ipragliflozin showed significantly greater improvements in hepatic steatosis indices (FLI, HSI, NAFLD-LFS) and liver fibrosis index (APRI) compared to metformin at 24 weeks (PMID: 39806556).
- In a 24-month observational pilot study of children with NAFLD, metformin plus lifestyle intervention did not appear more effective than lifestyle intervention alone in reducing steatosis or improving liver histology (PMID: 18640473).
- In a randomized placebo-controlled trial of 63 NAFLD patients, metformin treatment was associated with significant decreases in pulse wave velocity, augmentation index, fasting glucose, triglycerides, and alkaline phosphatase, and a marginal decrease in alanine aminotransferase, suggesting some metabolic and vascular benefit but not a clear primary hepatic steatosis benefit (PMID: 21411114).

#### Clinical Trials

**Development stage:** Phase 3 completed for this indication
Metformin has been evaluated across a broad clinical program in nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), spanning Phase 1 through Phase 3. At the pivotal level, the completed Phase 3 TONIC trial (NCT00063635) examined treatment of NAFLD in children, and a separate completed Phase 3 study (NCT05521633) compared metformin and pioglitazone on liver enzymes and ultrasound changes in non-diabetic NAFLD patients. A completed Phase 2/Phase 3 trial (NCT03129113) investigated adding maraviroc and/or metformin for hepatic steatosis in people living with HIV. Earlier-phase work includes two completed Phase 2 studies: NCT00063232, which evaluated metformin for NASH, and NCT01523639, a placebo-controlled Phase 2 study of metformin to prevent chemotherapy-associated steatohepatitis that was stopped early due to slow recruitment. A Phase 2 study (NCT02457286) was withdrawn because patients declined to participate. Several Phase 4 studies have also completed, including NCT03068065 examining antidiabetic effects on intrahepatic fat and NCT02147925 comparing liraglutide, sitagliptin, and glargine on liver fat in type 2 diabetes. One Phase 4 trial (NCT00736385) was terminated and another (NCT02500147) was stopped due to poor enrollment, funding, and administrative challenges — neither for safety or efficacy concerns. A number of additional Phase 2, Phase 4, and non-interventional studies carry unknown status. Two non-pivotal studies are currently recruiting: NCT06591169, a lifestyle-change study in PCOS with OSA and NAFLD, and NCT05275608, examining effects of a very-low-calorie ketogenic diet in metabolic syndrome.
