## Findings by Disease

### Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

#### Literature — Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

**Evidence strength:** moderate, supports
**Relevant studies:** 2

Two clinical studies of semaglutide in adults with type 1 diabetes provide evidence supporting its repurposing for this condition. A 26-week double-blind RCT (PMID: 40550013) found that once-weekly semaglutide significantly improved a composite endpoint of time-in-range >70%, time-below-range <4%, and ≥5% weight reduction compared to placebo (36% vs. 0%) in adults with type 1 diabetes and obesity using automated insulin delivery. A post-hoc analysis of a double-blind randomized crossover trial (PMID: 41144928) demonstrated that semaglutide rapidly reduced total daily insulin requirements and carbohydrate intake in type 1 diabetes patients on insulin pump therapy, with manageable hypoglycemia risk when using diabetes technology.

**Key findings:**
- In a 26-week double-blind RCT, 36% of adults with type 1 diabetes and obesity receiving semaglutide achieved the composite primary endpoint (CGM time-in-range >70%, time-below-range <4%, and ≥5% weight reduction) versus 0% in the placebo group (p<0.001) (PMID: 40550013)
- Semaglutide reduced body weight by a least-squares mean of -8.8 kg compared to placebo over 26 weeks in adults with type 1 diabetes and obesity (PMID: 40550013)
- Semaglutide improved CGM time-in-range (70–180 mg/dL) by 8.8 percentage points and reduced HbA1c by 0.3 percentage points versus placebo in adults with type 1 diabetes (PMID: 40550013)
- No diabetic ketoacidosis was reported and severe hypoglycemia events were equal between semaglutide and placebo groups (2 each) in the 26-week type 1 diabetes RCT (PMID: 40550013)
- In a randomized crossover trial post-hoc analysis, daily total and bolus insulin requirements were significantly reduced by day 7 of semaglutide initiation in type 1 diabetes patients on insulin pump therapy, with basal insulin significantly reduced by day 32 (PMID: 41144928)
- By day 77 of semaglutide use in type 1 diabetes pump therapy patients, carbohydrate ratios increased by a median of 4.1%, correction factors by 11.2%, and pre-programmed basal rates decreased by 7.9%, indicating need for pump parameter adjustments (PMID: 41144928)
- Median time spent in hypoglycemia was rarely >4% during follow-up visits in type 1 diabetes patients using semaglutide with diabetes technology (PMID: 41144928)

#### Clinical Trials

**Development stage:** Phase 2 completed for this indication, no Phase 3
Semaglutide has been evaluated across multiple phases of clinical investigation in nonalcoholic/metabolic fatty liver disease. At the foundational level, a completed Phase 1 study (NCT03357380, 'A Study on How Semaglutide Works on Early Stages of Scar Tissue in the Liver Assessed by Pictures of the Liver') characterized its early mechanistic effects on hepatic fibrosis. Building on this, two Phase 2 trials have completed: NCT04216589 ('The SLIM LIVER Study'), which examined semaglutide in NAFLD in the context of metabolic syndrome with insulin resistance and elevated cardiovascular risk, and a Phase 4 study NCT05813249 ('Semaglutide in Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease') that has also completed. A second Phase 2 study, NCT06005012, examining semaglutide in real-world fibrosis due to NAFLD in obesity and type 2 diabetes, carries an unknown current status. On the active side, NCT03884075 ('NAFLD HEROES') is a Phase 2 trial currently recruiting to assess hepatic glucose response and the effect of semaglutide in NAFLD. NCT05067621 is an active, non-recruiting Phase 2 study evaluating semaglutide effects in obese youth with prediabetes or new-onset type 2 diabetes and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease. NCT06374875 ('Fibrosis Lessens After Metabolic Surgery') is a recruiting Phase 4 trial examining fibrosis outcomes in a surgical context that includes semaglutide. Finally, NCT07570810, a not-yet-recruiting study of not-applicable phase, will assess the efficacy, safety, and tolerability of CS0159 combined with semaglutide in MAFLD patients with obesity and type 2 diabetes.
