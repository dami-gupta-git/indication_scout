## Findings by Disease

### Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

#### Literature — Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials demonstrate that metformin improves key features of polycystic ovary syndrome, including menstrual frequency, hormonal profiles (reduced LH, FSH, testosterone, androstenedione), and insulin sensitivity (PMID: 10634377, PMID: 27813052, PMID: 17766923, PMID: 22309675). Evidence is mixed regarding IVF outcomes, where metformin reduced the number of retrieved oocytes without improving live birth rate (PMID: 29576001), and benefits in non-obese women were not consistently demonstrated (PMID: 17766923). Overall, the evidence supports repurposing metformin for PCOS, particularly for improving menstrual regularity, ovulation, and androgen excess, though effects vary by patient subgroup and outcome.

**Key findings:**
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled 6-month trial, metformin (500 mg tid) improved mean menstrual frequency (p=0.002), reduced serum free testosterone (p<0.05), and increased insulin sensitivity (p<0.05) in women with PCOS, with 54.8% of subjects showing striking improvements in menstrual abnormalities in the subsequent open long-term trial (PMID: 10634377).
- In a randomized placebo-controlled trial of 40 PCOS patients, metformin (500 mg tid for 3 months) significantly reduced serum LH (p<0.001), FSH (p<0.01), visfatin (p<0.001), and systolic and diastolic blood pressures (p<0.001) compared to placebo (PMID: 27813052).
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled crossover trial of 56 women with PCOS, metformin (850 mg twice daily for 6 months) reduced weight and systolic blood pressure and increased HDL; in obese women specifically, it also reduced testosterone (p=0.013), fasting plasma glucose (p=0.018), insulin (p=0.045), and HOMA index (p=0.022), though non-obese women did not benefit (PMID: 17766923).
- In a prospective randomized trial comparing metformin to lifestyle changes in 40 PCOS women over 6 months, both interventions improved menstrual pattern by approximately 67%, with BMI reduction identified as the predictor of menstrual pattern improvement (PMID: 22309675).
- In a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled IVF trial of 102 overweight/obese PCOS women, metformin (1000 mg/day) decreased the mean number of retrieved oocytes (9.06 vs 16.86, p<0.01) and fertilized oocytes (5.65 vs 9, p<0.01) compared to placebo, but did not improve live birth rate (25.5% vs 17.6%, p=0.34) or implantation rate (PMID: 29576001).
- No cases of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) were observed in either the metformin or placebo group in the IVF trial of overweight/obese PCOS women (PMID: 29576001).
- In the open long-term metformin trial, among subjects who achieved regular menses, 79% of assessed cycles (32 out of 39) became ovulatory (PMID: 10634377).

### Metabolic Dysfunction-associated Steatotic Liver Disease

#### Literature — Metabolic Dysfunction-associated Steatotic Liver Disease

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple randomized controlled trials have examined metformin for NAFLD/MASLD, yielding mixed results. Metformin failed to improve liver histology (steatosis, NAS score) versus placebo in a 6-month RCT (PMID: 19811343), and did not significantly improve liver steatosis by FibroScan CAP/kPa scores in a 4-arm RCT (PMID: 41828627). However, metformin improved arterial properties, liver enzymes, and metabolic parameters in another RCT (PMID: 21411114), and reduced intrahepatic fat content and improved liver function in a comparative RCT (PMID: 28332301); a sub-analysis also showed metformin produced some improvement in hepatic steatosis indices, though less than ipragliflozin (PMID: 39806556).

**Key findings:**
- In a 6-month randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 44 patients with biopsy-proven NAFLD, metformin showed no significant difference versus placebo in liver steatosis assessed histologically or by CT, NAS score, or liver transaminases, though it did reduce body weight, cholesterol, LDL, glucose, and HbA1c (PMID: 19811343).
- In a 4-arm randomized placebo-controlled trial of 64 MASLD patients, metformin alone did not produce statistically significant improvements in liver steatosis by CAP or kPa scores at 6 months, whereas Ganwei alone did (PMID: 41828627).
- In a randomized placebo-controlled trial of 63 NAFLD patients, metformin treatment significantly decreased pulse wave velocity and augmentation index, reduced fasting glucose and triglycerides, decreased alkaline phosphatase, and increased HDL cholesterol, with a marginal decrease in ALT and increase in adiponectin (PMID: 21411114).
- In a 24-week RCT of 87 T2DM patients with NAFLD, metformin reduced HbA1c levels, reduced intrahepatic fat content, reduced weight, and improved liver function, though liraglutide achieved slightly better improvements in intrahepatic fat content reduction (PMID: 28332301).
- In a 24-week sub-analysis of a randomized controlled study in T2DM patients, metformin produced smaller improvements in hepatic steatosis indices (FLI, HSI, NAFLD-LFS) and liver fibrosis index (APRI) compared with ipragliflozin (PMID: 39806556).

### Coronary Artery Disorder

#### Literature — Coronary Artery Disorder

**Evidence strength:** strong, supports
**Relevant studies:** 3

Multiple clinical trials support the use of metformin in patients with coronary artery disease (CAD). A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (PMID: 30993313) demonstrated that metformin significantly reduced left ventricular mass index, body weight, systolic blood pressure, and oxidative stress in non-diabetic CAD patients with insulin resistance and/or pre-diabetes. A multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (PMID: 23230096) showed that metformin reduced major cardiovascular events by 46% compared to glipizide in type 2 diabetic patients with CAD over a median follow-up of 5 years. The MET-REMODEL study design (PMID: 25545400) further contextualizes the rationale for investigating metformin's ability to regress left ventricular hypertrophy in CAD patients, supporting the overall evidence that metformin may benefit patients with coronary artery disease.

**Key findings:**
- In a randomized placebo-controlled trial of 68 non-diabetic CAD patients with insulin resistance and/or pre-diabetes, metformin XL (2000 mg daily) significantly reduced left ventricular mass indexed to height1.7 (LVMI) compared to placebo (absolute mean difference -1.37, 95% CI: -2.63 to -0.12, P=0.033) over 12 months (PMID: 30993313).
- Metformin treatment also significantly reduced LVM (P=0.032), body weight (P=0.001), subcutaneous adipose tissue (P=0.024), office systolic blood pressure (P=0.022), and oxidative stress biomarker TBARS (P=0.04) compared to placebo in CAD patients (PMID: 30993313).
- In a multicenter, randomized, double-blind trial of 304 type 2 diabetic patients with CAD, metformin reduced the composite of major cardiovascular events (death from cardiovascular cause, death from any cause, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or arterial revascularization) with an adjusted hazard ratio of 0.54 (95% CI 0.30-0.90; P=0.026) compared to glipizide over a median follow-up of 5.0 years (PMID: 23230096).
- The MET-REMODEL study was designed as a single-center, phase IV, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial to investigate metformin's efficacy in regressing LVH in CAD patients with insulin resistance, with primary endpoint of change in left ventricular mass index (PMID: 25545400).
- The MET-REMODEL study rationale identified insulin resistance and central obesity as key risk factors for LVH in CAD patients, and proposed that metformin's reduction of insulin resistance and weight loss could regress LVH independent of blood pressure control (PMID: 25545400).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [23230096](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23230096/), [30993313](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30993313/)
