## Findings by Disease

### Attention Deficit-hyperactivity Disorder

#### Literature — Attention Deficit-hyperactivity Disorder

**Evidence strength:** strong, supports
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled and active-comparator trials demonstrate that bupropion is effective for treating ADHD in both children and adults. In adults, bupropion SR produced a 42% reduction in ADHD symptoms versus 24% for placebo (PMID: 11156812), bupropion XL showed 53% responders versus 31% for placebo (PMID: 15820237), and a 6-week trial found significant CAARS score improvement over placebo (PMID: 25325205). In pediatric and mixed-age populations, bupropion demonstrated significant effects on hyperactivity and conduct problems versus placebo (PMID: 8885585) and produced equivalent improvement to methylphenidate on multiple outcome measures (PMID: 7775360). The evidence supports repurposing bupropion for ADHD.

**Key findings:**
- In a 6-week double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT in adults with DSM-IV ADHD, bupropion SR produced a 42% reduction in ADHD symptoms versus 24% for placebo, with 76% of bupropion-treated subjects responding versus 37% on placebo (PMID: 11156812).
- In a multisite, 8-week placebo-controlled RCT of bupropion XL (up to 450 mg/day) in 162 adults with ADHD, 53% of bupropion XL-treated patients were responders versus 31% on placebo (p=.004), with a treatment effect size of 0.6 on the ADHD Rating Scale (PMID: 15820237).
- In a 6-week double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in 42 adults with ADHD, bupropion 150 mg/day produced significantly greater reductions in CAARS scores compared to placebo at week 6 (PMID: 25325205).
- In a multisite, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in children aged 6–12 with ADDH (n=109), bupropion 3–6 mg/kg/day showed significant treatment effects on hyperactivity and conduct problems on teacher and parent Conners scales, as well as on the Continuous Performance Test and memory retrieval test (PMID: 8885585).
- In a double-blind crossover trial comparing bupropion to methylphenidate in 15 subjects aged 7–17 with ADHD, both drugs produced significantly greater and equivalent improvement on the Iowa-Conners Teacher's Rating Scale, Clinical Global Impression Scale, and multiple cognitive and behavioral measures (PMID: 7775360).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [11156812](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11156812/), [8885585](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8885585/), [15820237](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15820237/), [7775360](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7775360/), [25325205](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25325205/)

⚠️ **Indication-specific safety:** Multiple abstracts report safety findings in ADHD patients treated with bupropion, including a randomized placebo-controlled trial in adults with ADHD that explicitly evaluated safety (PMID: [15820237](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15820237/)), blood pressure changes in adults with ADHD on bupropion (PMID: [15705013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15705013/)), tolerability findings in adolescents with comorbid ADHD and depression (PMID: [11288772](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11288772/)), and systematic reviews reporting harms/tolerability of bupropion specifically in ADHD populations (PMIDs: [30097390](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30097390/), [18026719](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18026719/), [19621975](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19621975/)).

### Mood Disorder

#### Literature — Mood Disorder

**Evidence strength:** moderate, supports
**Relevant studies:** 2

The evidence supports repurposing bupropion for mood disorders beyond its approved indications of MDD and SAD, specifically for bipolar and schizoaffective disorders. An open trial found that bupropion maintained 11 patients with bipolar or schizoaffective disorder for 1 year or more with little or no relapse and few side effects (PMID: 2856918). A pharmacokinetic study in inpatients with mood disorders noted that bupropion may be less likely than other antidepressants to cause switches into mania and rapid cycling, suggesting utility in bipolar disorder, though it also identified a clinically significant drug interaction with carbamazepine that markedly reduces bupropion exposure (PMID: 8830063).

**Key findings:**
- In an open trial of 11 patients with bipolar or schizoaffective disorder (most intolerant of or minimally improved on lithium, neuroleptics, or antidepressants), bupropion alone or combined with low-dose neuroleptics or anxiolytics produced little or no relapse over 1 year or more with few side effects (PMID: 2856918).
- Bupropion was noted to be potentially less likely than other antidepressants to cause switches into mania and rapid cycling, suggesting utility in bipolar disorder (PMID: 8830063).
- Carbamazepine co-administration in inpatients with mood disorders decreased bupropion peak concentrations by 87% and 24-hour AUC by 90%, representing a clinically significant drug interaction relevant to combination therapy in mood disorders (PMID: 8830063).
- Valproate did not significantly affect bupropion pharmacokinetics in inpatients with mood disorders, suggesting it may be a safer combination partner than carbamazepine (PMID: 8830063).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [2856918](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2856918/)

**Context (non-efficacy) PMIDs:** [8830063](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8830063/)

⚠️ **Indication-specific safety:** Multiple abstracts report safety findings in mood disorder contexts, including mood switch/mania induction in bipolar depression, seizures in depressed patients, treatment-emergent mania in rapid cycling bipolar disorder, neuropsychiatric adverse events in mood disorder patients in EAGLES, emotional blunting in major depressive disorder trials, and periodic limb movements during sleep in adolescents treated with bupropion for mood disorder.

**Indication-specific safety PMIDs:** [16880481](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16880481/), [9714265](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9714265/), [1744061](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1744061/), [17392295](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17392295/), [19594506](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19594506/), [17017819](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17017819/), [30811371](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30811371/), [16262594](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16262594/), [36029876](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36029876/), [36073843](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36073843/)

### Cocaine Use Disorder

#### Literature — Cocaine Use Disorder

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 2

Two randomized controlled trials examined bupropion for cocaine use disorder with conflicting results. In a human laboratory study, naltrexone-bupropion combinations did not significantly decrease cocaine self-administration on either a progressive ratio or relapse procedure, and the authors concluded these combinations do not support treatment of cocaine use disorder (PMID: 36805862). In a 26-week placebo-controlled RCT, bupropion SR combined with financial abstinence incentives showed no overall benefit versus placebo, but a sensitivity analysis found that participants in the abstinence initiation subgroup had significantly better cocaine abstinence outcomes with bupropion SR than placebo (PMID: 36920397).

**Key findings:**
- Naltrexone and bupropion alone or in combination did not significantly decrease cocaine self-administration on either a progressive ratio or relapse procedure in a human laboratory study (PMID: 36805862).
- Low doses of bupropion (100 mg) blunted cocaine-induced subjective measures of 'Like Drug' and 'Stimulated' without unexpected adverse effects (PMID: 36805862).
- No significant overall difference in urinalysis or self-reported cocaine use was observed between bupropion SR and placebo groups in a 26-week RCT (PMID: 36920397).
- In the abstinence initiation subgroup, participants receiving bupropion SR had a significantly higher mean number of cocaine-negative samples than placebo (30.3 vs 17.1; P=.05) and more were abstinent at end of study (66.7% vs 30.0%; P=.04) (PMID: 36920397).
- Participants in the relapse prevention subgroup had high abstinence (>80%) regardless of whether they received bupropion SR or placebo (PMID: 36920397).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [36920397](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36920397/)

**Contradicting PMIDs:** [36805862](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36805862/), [36920397](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36920397/)

⚠️ **Indication-specific safety:** PMID: [36805862](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36805862/) explicitly evaluated safety and tolerability of naltrexone-bupropion in cocaine use disorder patients, and PMID: [40981132](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40981132/) is a case report of combined naltrexone-bupropion therapy specifically for cocaine use disorder, both reporting safety/tolerability findings in the cocaine use disorder context.

**Indication-specific safety PMIDs:** [36805862](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36805862/), [40981132](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40981132/)
