## Findings by Disease

### Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder

#### Literature — Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder

**Evidence strength:** strong, supports
**Relevant studies:** 4

Multiple randomized, controlled trials demonstrate that bupropion is effective for treating ADHD. In adults, bupropion SR produced a 42% reduction in ADHD symptoms versus 24% for placebo (PMID: 11156812), and bupropion XL showed a 53% responder rate versus 31% for placebo with an effect size of 0.6 (PMID: 15820237). In children, a multisite double-blind trial found significant treatment effects on hyperactivity and conduct problems (PMID: 8885585), and a crossover trial found bupropion equivalent to methylphenidate in overall ADHD efficacy (PMID: 7775360). PMID 17685748 was excluded as it studied bupropion for smoking prevention in ADHD patients, not for treating ADHD itself.

**Key findings:**
- In a 6-week double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT in adults with DSM-IV ADHD, bupropion SR produced a 42% reduction in ADHD symptoms versus 24% for placebo, with 76% of bupropion-treated subjects achieving ≥30% symptom reduction compared to 37% on placebo (PMID: 11156812).
- In a multisite 8-week placebo-controlled trial of bupropion XL (up to 450 mg/day) in 162 adults with ADHD, 53% of bupropion XL-treated patients were responders versus 31% on placebo (p=.004), with a treatment effect size of 0.6 on the ADHD Rating Scale (PMID: 15820237).
- Bupropion XL provided sustained benefit throughout the day compared with placebo (morning p=.033, afternoon p=.004, evening p=.024) and was well tolerated with a 5% drug-related discontinuation rate (PMID: 15820237).
- In a multisite double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in children aged 6–12 with ADDH, bupropion (3–6 mg/kg/day) showed significant treatment effects on hyperactivity and conduct problems on teacher ratings as early as day 3, and on parent ratings at day 28 (PMID: 8885585).
- In a double-blind crossover trial in 15 subjects aged 7–17 with ADHD, bupropion and methylphenidate both produced significantly greater improvement (p<.001) on the Iowa-Conners Teacher's Rating Scale and did not differ in overall efficacy (PMID: 7775360).
- The crossover trial also found equivalent improvement for bupropion and methylphenidate on the Clinical Global Impression Scale, Continuous Performance Test, and cognitive measures including the Rey Auditory-Verbal Learning Test (PMID: 7775360).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [11156812](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11156812/), [8885585](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8885585/), [15820237](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15820237/), [7775360](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7775360/)

### Cocaine Dependence

#### Literature — Cocaine Dependence

**Evidence strength:** strong, mixed
**Relevant studies:** 5

Multiple clinical trials have examined bupropion for cocaine dependence, yielding inconsistent results. A randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial found no significant differences between bupropion and placebo on cocaine use outcomes when combined with cognitive behavioral therapy (PMID: 18551884), and bupropion was not more effective than placebo for cocaine abstinence in opioid-dependent patients on buprenorphine (PMID: 18612883). However, a 25-week placebo-controlled trial suggested that combining bupropion with contingency management significantly reduced cocaine-positive urine samples compared to contingency management with placebo (PMID: 16461866), a small open-label study found improvement in drug abuse screening scores with bupropion add-on therapy in bipolar patients with comorbid cocaine dependence (PMID: 24434527), and an experimental within-subject study showed acute bupropion reduced preference for cocaine versus money while also enhancing some positive subjective effects of cocaine (PMID: 22168398).

**Key findings:**
- A randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial (N=70) found no statistically significant differences between bupropion and placebo in urine drug screen results, treatment retention, cocaine craving, or depressive symptoms when combined with cognitive behavioral therapy (PMID: 18551884).
- In a 25-week placebo-controlled RCT (N=106), the group receiving both contingency management and bupropion showed a significant decrease in cocaine-positive urine samples during weeks 3-13, whereas the contingency management plus placebo group showed a significant increase in cocaine use during the same period (PMID: 16461866).
- In a double-blind placebo-controlled trial of opioid-dependent smokers on buprenorphine (N=40), bupropion was not more effective than placebo for abstinence from cocaine, and treatment retention was significantly lower in the bupropion group (58%) versus placebo (90%) (PMID: 18612883).
- In a small open-label randomized study of 12 depressed bipolar I patients with comorbid cocaine dependence, bupropion add-on therapy was associated with improvement in Drug Abuse Screening Test scores compared to the comparison group after 4 weeks (PMID: 24434527).
- In a within-subject placebo-controlled experiment (N=8), acute bupropion pre-treatment reduced the number of choices for 45 mg intranasal cocaine versus money (from 5/6 to 3.13-4/6 choices) but also consistently enhanced positive subject-rated effects of cocaine (PMID: 22168398).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [24434527](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24434527/), [16461866](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16461866/), [22168398](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22168398/)

**Contradicting PMIDs:** [18551884](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18551884/), [18612883](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18612883/)

### Mood Disorder

#### Literature — Mood Disorder

**Evidence strength:** moderate, supports
**Relevant studies:** 2

The evidence supports repurposing bupropion for mood disorders beyond its approved indications, specifically for bipolar and schizoaffective disorders. An open trial found that bupropion maintained 11 patients with bipolar or schizoaffective disorder for 1 year or more with little or no relapse and few side effects (PMID: 2856918). A pharmacokinetic study in inpatients with mood disorders noted that bupropion may be less likely than other antidepressants to cause switches into mania and rapid cycling, suggesting utility in bipolar disorder, though it also identified a clinically significant drug interaction with carbamazepine that markedly reduces bupropion exposure (PMID: 8830063).

**Key findings:**
- In an open trial of 11 patients with bipolar or schizoaffective disorder (most intolerant of or minimally improved on lithium, neuroleptics, or antidepressants), bupropion alone or combined with low-dose neuroleptics or anxiolytics produced little or no relapse over 1 year or more with few side effects (PMID: 2856918).
- Bupropion was noted to be potentially less likely than other antidepressants to cause switches into mania and rapid cycling, suggesting utility in bipolar disorder (PMID: 8830063).
- Carbamazepine co-administration in inpatients with mood disorders decreased bupropion peak concentrations by 87% and 24-hour AUC by 90%, representing a clinically important drug interaction relevant to combination therapy in mood disorders (PMID: 8830063).
- Valproate did not significantly affect bupropion concentrations in inpatients with mood disorders, suggesting it may be a safer combination partner than carbamazepine (PMID: 8830063).

**Supporting PMIDs:** [2856918](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2856918/)

**Context (non-efficacy) PMIDs:** [8830063](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8830063/)
